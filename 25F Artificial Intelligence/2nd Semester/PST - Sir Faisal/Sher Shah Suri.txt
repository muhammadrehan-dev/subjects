Sher Shah Suri’s Reforms
The road network was repaired, which flourished local and international trade and led to new expansion. Trees were also planted along the roads.

A number of rest houses and inns were built for travellers at every eight kilometres. Wells were also dug for them. Food was also provided to the poor and needy.

In the history of the subcontinent, he introduced, for the first time, the system of measurement of agricultural land, and production records were also maintained.

He also introduced, for the first time, the Dak system. Through it, horses carried mail or post.

He established madrasas attached to educational institutions.

For the first time, customs and excise duties were imposed on all imported and exported goods, and all internal taxes were abolished.

He organized the army and strictly checked it himself. Looting and killings were punished after any victory.

An intelligence system was introduced to monitor the activities of officials and rebels.

He also divided the kingdom into provinces. Every province had sarkars (districts), parganas (small units), and villages. This decentralization of power was introduced in South Asia for the first time.

The coin, or rupiya, replaced the old mixed-metal currency.

Law and order was excellently maintained during Sher Shah’s reign. Criminal activities were reduced to almost zero.
